Navigating Puppy Chewing: When and What Kind of Treats to Offer

Puppy Chewing Puppy Training Dog Treats
Puppyhood is an exciting time, filled with boundless energy and adorable antics. One of the most common, and sometimes challenging, aspects of this phase is puppy chewing. Understanding when and how to use treats can be a game-changer for redirecting this natural behavior positively.

Quick Answer:

Offer treats during positive reinforcement training sessions, as rewards for good behavior (like choosing an appropriate chew toy), and to redirect chewing from forbidden items. Small, highly palatable treats like Brutus & Barnaby Training Treats are ideal for frequent rewarding.

Why Do Puppies Chew? Understanding the "Why"

Chewing is a natural and essential behavior for puppies. It's how they explore their world, relieve teething discomfort, and learn about their environment. From around 3 weeks old, puppies start exploring with their mouths, and this intensifies as they get their adult teeth. This phase can last well into their first year.

  • Exploration: Puppies use their mouths to discover textures, tastes, and objects.
  • Teething Relief: Just like human babies, puppies experience discomfort as their teeth come in. Chewing helps soothe their gums.
  • Boredom/Energy: Puppies have a lot of energy and need outlets. Chewing can be a way to self-soothe or pass the time.
  • Instinct: It's a deeply ingrained behavior that helps them learn and practice skills.

When to Offer Treats for Puppy Chewing

Treats are powerful tools for guiding your puppy's behavior. The key is timing and intention. You want to reward desired behaviors and redirect unwanted ones.

1. During Training Sessions

Puppy training is crucial, and treats are a cornerstone of positive reinforcement. Use small, high-value treats to reward your puppy for learning commands like "sit," "stay," or "leave it." This builds a positive association with learning and with you.

2. Redirecting Chewing

When you catch your puppy chewing something inappropriate (like a shoe), don't scold. Instead, calmly interrupt them, remove the item, and immediately offer an appropriate chew toy or a Brutus & Barnaby Training Treat as a distraction. Praise them when they engage with the appropriate item.

3. Rewarding Calmness and Appropriate Choices

Did your puppy just pick up their designated chew toy instead of the sofa leg? Reward that! Offer praise and a small treat to reinforce that they made a good choice. This is especially helpful during crate training or quiet times.

4. Socialization and New Experiences

Introducing your puppy to new environments, sounds, or people can be overwhelming. Having a few tasty treats on hand can help create positive associations and build confidence.

Choosing the Right Treats: Key Factors

When selecting treats for your puppy, especially considering their chewing habits, keep these points in mind:

  • Size and Portability: For training, treats should be small enough to be given frequently without overfeeding. They should also be easy to break into smaller pieces if needed.
  • Ingredients: Opt for natural ingredients with no artificial colors, flavors, or preservatives. Look for protein sources as the primary ingredients.
  • Nutritional Value: While treats are supplementary, choose options that offer some nutritional benefit. Puppy-specific treats are often formulated for their developmental needs.
  • Palatability: Treats should be enticing enough to hold your puppy's attention during training or to distract them from unwanted chewing.
  • Digestibility: Puppies can have sensitive stomachs. Choose treats that are easily digestible and consider single-protein options if your puppy has known sensitivities.

The Role of Chews vs. Training Treats

It's important to differentiate between training treats and chews. Training treats are typically small, soft, and consumed quickly, making them perfect for immediate rewards during training. Chews, on the other hand, are designed to be long-lasting, providing an outlet for chewing behavior, teething relief, and mental stimulation.

For redirecting destructive chewing, a longer-lasting chew is often more effective than a small training treat. However, training treats are invaluable for reinforcing good behavior and teaching your puppy what they *should* be doing.

When to Use Longer-Lasting Chews:

  • When your puppy needs a distraction and mental engagement.
  • As a reward for being calm and settling down.
  • To satisfy their natural urge to chew for extended periods.
  • As a rawhide alternative.

When to Use Small Training Treats:

  • During obedience training sessions.
  • To reward quick, desirable actions (e.g., pottying outside).
  • For positive reinforcement in new or slightly stressful situations.
  • As a very high-value reward for difficult commands or when a significant distraction is present.

Important Considerations: Moderation and Supervision

While treats are fantastic tools, moderation is key to preventing health issues like obesity. The general guideline is that treats should not make up more than 10% of your dog's daily caloric intake. Always consult your veterinarian if you're unsure about how many treats are appropriate for your puppy's specific needs, diet, and activity level. (AKC: How many treats can a dog have?)

For longer-lasting chews, supervision is paramount. Ensure the chew is appropriate for your puppy's size and chewing strength to prevent choking or blockages. Remove any chew that becomes small enough to swallow whole or has sharp edges. Always ensure fresh water is available.

When to Consult a Vet

While chewing is normal, sudden changes in behavior, excessive chewing, or chewing accompanied by other symptoms like lethargy, loss of appetite, vomiting, or diarrhea, warrant a veterinary visit. Your vet can rule out any underlying medical issues, dental problems, or behavioral concerns. (ASPCA: Common dog behavior issues)

Frequently Asked Questions About Puppy Chewing and Treats

Q: How do I stop my puppy from chewing furniture?

Redirect your puppy to an appropriate chew toy and praise them when they engage with it. Ensure they have plenty of appropriate chew options available and are getting enough exercise and mental stimulation. Puppy-proofing your home by removing tempting items is also helpful.

Q: Can I give my puppy adult dog treats?

It's generally best to use puppy-specific treats or very small, easily digestible adult treats. Puppies have different nutritional needs and potentially more sensitive digestive systems. Always check the ingredients and consult your veterinarian.

Q: How often should I give my puppy treats?

For training, frequent small treats are effective. However, be mindful of their total daily caloric intake to prevent weight gain. Treats should supplement, not replace, their regular meals. Consider their overall diet and activity level.

Q: What's the difference between a training treat and a chew?

Training treats are small, soft, and quickly consumed, used for immediate rewards during training. Chews are larger, more durable, and designed for extended chewing to satisfy natural instincts and provide mental stimulation.

Q: My puppy is teething and chewing everything. What can I do?

Provide plenty of safe, puppy-appropriate chew toys, including ones that can be chilled or frozen for extra relief. Redirect chewing from inappropriate items to these toys and offer praise and a treat when they chew their own toys.
